Section 1 Rates of Change

Subsection 1.1 Performance Criteria

  1. Determine the average rates of change of a function algebraically from its expression.

  2. Determine the average rate of change of a function from its graph.

  3. Calculate average velocity from a set of data points over an interval.

  4. Calculate an estimate for the instantaneous velocity at a point from a set of data points.

  5. Approximate the instantaneous rate of change(or slope of the graph, or derivative) of a function at a point, from its graph.

Subsection 1.2 Average Rate of Change

Example 1.1.

Suppose that while climbing Mt. Shasta you note the temperature at the bottom and at the top of the mountain. Mt. Shasta is roughly 14,000 feet above sea level. At the base of the mountain (5,000 feet above sea level) the temperature is \(45^{\circ}\)F, while at the summit, the temperature is \(-15^{\circ}\)F. What is the average rate of change of temperature while climbing?

Solution.

It's easy to see that the temperature is a function of the height (the higher you go, the colder it gets). So, we first want to find out the change in temperature between the two points (base(\(t_b\)) and summit(\(t_s\))). The change is

\begin{equation*} t_s-t_b = -15^{\circ}\text{F}-45^{\circ}\text{F}= -60^{\circ}\text{F}. \end{equation*}

However, you are asked to find the {\bf average} rate of change of temperature while you are climbing. The temperature changes due to change in height which is

\begin{equation*} h_s - h_b = 14,000\text{ ft.} - 5,000\text{ ft.} = 9,000\text{ ft.} \end{equation*}

Therefore, the average rate of change of temperature is the change in temperature with respect to the height i.e.

\begin{equation*} \textbf{average rate of change } = \frac{t_s-t_b}{h_s-h_b} = \frac{-60^{\circ}\text{F}}{9,000\text{ ft.}} = -0.0067^{\circ}\text{F}/\text{foot} \end{equation*}

In the previous example the temperature(\(t\)) was a function of the height(\(h\)). Therefore, \(t\) was the dependent variable and \(h\) was the independent variable. In general,

\begin{equation*} \textbf{Average rate of change} = \frac{\textbf{Change in the dependent variable}}{\textbf{Change in the independent variable}} \end{equation*}

Subsection 1.3 Average Rate of Change of a Function

Consider a function \(y = f(x)\text{.}\) Recall that \(x\) is the independent variable and \(y\) is the dependent variable. Also consider two points \(x_1\) and \(x_2\) in the domain of \(f\) with \(x_2>x_1\text{.}\) Let \(y_1=f(x_1)\) and \(y_2=f(x_2)\text{.}\) Then going by the previous definition of the average rate of change we have,

Definition 1.2.

For a function \(f(x)\) with points \(x_1, x_2\) (\(x_2 > x_1\)) in the domain of \(f\text{,}\) the average rate of change of f with respect to> \(x\) over the interval \([x_1, x_2]\) is

\begin{equation*} \frac{\Delta f}{\Delta x} = \frac{f(x_2)-f(x_1)}{x_2-x_1} \end{equation*}

Here \(\Delta f\) and \(\Delta x\) denote the change in \(f\) and \(x\) respectively.

Example 1.3.

Consider the function \(f(x) = -2x^2 + 4x -1\text{.}\) Find the average rate of change of \(f\) with respect to \(x\) from \(x=0\) to \(x=1\text{.}\)

Solution.

Here \(x_1 = 0\) and \(x_2=1\text{.}\) Therefore,

\begin{equation*} \frac{\Delta f}{\Delta x} = \frac{f(1)-f(0)}{1-0} = \frac{1-(-1)}{1}=2 \end{equation*}

Subsection 1.4 Average Velocity

Consider a distance function \(s(t)\) which denotes the distance of a particle from the starting point at time \(t\text{.}\) Between the time interval \([t_1, t_2]\text{,}\) the average rate of change of the function \(s(t)\) with respect to \(t\) is defined to be the average velocity of the particle, i.e.

Definition 1.4.

The average velocity of a moving particle with distance function \(s(t)\) between the time interval \([t_1, t_2]\) is

\begin{equation*} \frac{\Delta s}{\Delta t} = \frac{s(t_2)-s(t_1)}{t_2-t_1} \end{equation*}

Example 1.5.

A ball dropped from a state of rest at time \(t=0\) travels a distance \(s(t)=4.9t^2\) m in \(t\) seconds. Compute its average velocity over the time interval \([3, 3.5]\text{.}\)

Solution.

Here \(t_1 = 3\) and \(t_2=3.5\text{.}\) The average velocity between these two points is therefore,

\begin{equation*} \frac{\Delta s}{\Delta t} = \frac{s(3.5)-s(3)}{3.5-3}=\frac{60.025-44.1}{0.5} \text{ m/s}= 31.85 \text{ m/s}. \end{equation*}

Subsection 1.6 Instataneous Rate of Change

Instantaneous rate of change is similar to the average rate of change that we looked at earlier but over “very small” intervals. This is a very vague way to describe instantaneous rate of change, therefore, let's look at the earlier Example 1.3

Example 1.8.

Consider the function \(f(x) = -2x^2 + 4x -1\text{.}\) Find the instantaneous rate of change of \(f\) at \(x=0\text{.}\)

Solution.

Here we have just one point. As we mentioned in our “vague” definition, let's consider the small interval \([0, 0.1]\)>, and let's compute the average rate of change over this interval.

\begin{equation*} \frac{\Delta f}{\Delta x} \Bigg |_{[0, 0.1]} = \frac{-0.62-(-1)}{0.1-0}=\frac{0.38}{0.1} = 3.8 \end{equation*}

Now let's look at an even smaller interval \([0, 0.01]\text{.}\) The average rate of change over this interval is,

\begin{equation*} \frac{\Delta f}{\Delta x} \Bigg |_{[0, 0.01]} = \frac{-0.9602-(-1)}{0.01-0}=\frac{0.0398}{0.01} = 3.98 \end{equation*}
