Solution 1.8.1.

Here we have just one point. As we mentioned in our “vague” definition, let's consider the small interval \([0, 0.1]\text{,}\) and let's compute the average rate of change over this interval.

\begin{equation*} \frac{\Delta f}{\Delta x} \Bigg |_{[0, 0.1]} = \frac{-0.62-(-1)}{0.1-0}=\frac{0.38}{0.1} = 3.8 \end{equation*}

Now let's look at an even smaller interval \([0, 0.01]\text{.}\) The average rate of change over this interval is,

\begin{equation*} \frac{\Delta f}{\Delta x} \Bigg |_{[0, 0.01]} = \frac{-0.9602-(-1)}{0.01-0}=\frac{0.0398}{0.01} = 3.98 \end{equation*}

We can keep on doing this for few more intervals each smaller than the previous. We construct a table involving these intervals.

The above table clearly shows that, as the intervals get smaller and smaller, the difference quotient \(\dfrac{\Delta f}{\Delta x}\) gets closer to 4. This suggests that 4 is a good candidate for the instantaneous rate of change of the function \(f\) at \(x=0\text{.}\)
